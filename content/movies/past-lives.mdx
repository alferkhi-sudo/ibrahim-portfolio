Celine Song's debut feature is a film of extraordinary restraint and devastating emotional power. Past Lives tells the story of Nora and Hae Sung, childhood sweethearts in Seoul who are separated when Nora's family emigrates to Canada. They reconnect twice — once online in their twenties, and once in person in their thirties, when Nora is married and living in New York.

What makes this film remarkable is everything it chooses not to do. There are no dramatic confrontations, no grand declarations, no villains. Instead, Song constructs a delicate meditation on the Korean concept of inyeon — the providential connections between people across lifetimes.

Greta Lee and Teo Yoo deliver performances of incredible subtlety. So much is communicated through glances, pauses, and the careful distance they maintain. The scene on the bench at the East River is among the most quietly devastating in recent cinema — two people acknowledging the weight of what might have been, without ever speaking it directly.

The film's genius lies in its structure: each act separated by twelve years, each reunion carrying the accumulated weight of time and choice. Song's camera observes these moments with patience and tenderness, never rushing toward a resolution that life itself would not provide.

Past Lives is a film about the space between people — the gap between who we were and who we've become, between the lives we live and the lives we imagine. It leaves you changed.
